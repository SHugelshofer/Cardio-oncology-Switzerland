## 1. General Background

### 1.1 A Major Clinical Challenge

Advances in oncology have transformed many cancers into chronic diseases: in Switzerland, more than 60% of patients survive beyond 5 years. This improvement has revealed a critical issue: the cardiovascular toxicity of anticancer therapies. The risk of death from cardiovascular disease is approximately doubled in cancer survivors compared with the general population, and the risk of developing heart failure is increased by about 50%.

Anthracyclines, anti-HER2 agents, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, and immune checkpoint inhibitors induce cardiotoxicity ranging from subclinical ventricular dysfunction to severe heart failure. In our cardio-oncology clinic (5–6 patients per day, 4–5 days per week since January 2025, i.e. approximately 1,000 patients per year), we encounter this problem on a daily basis. According to the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) cardio-oncology guidelines, the creation of cardio-oncology registries is strongly encouraged in order to monitor the quality of care and to contribute to clinical research in this field, which still lacks robust evidence-based recommendations.

### 1.2 The Critical Intersection of Cancer, Diabetes and Cardiovascular Comorbidities

Cardiovascular risk factors (CVRFs) play a key role in determining an individual patient's susceptibility to cardiotoxicity and may also increase the risk of developing certain cancers de novo. Patients with cancer frequently present with CVRFs, with prevalence varying according to cancer type and age distribution: **diabetes (20–30%), hypertension (35–50%), dyslipidaemia (30–40%), obesity (25–35%), and established cardiovascular disease (15–25%)**. CVRFs have a major influence on overall prognosis, making their systematic detection essential. Among these, **diabetes is particularly relevant in cardio-oncology**, as diabetic patients have a 2 to 3-fold higher risk of developing cardiac dysfunction under anthracycline therapy.

The 2022 ESC cardio-oncology guidelines recommend systematic evaluation and optimisation of CVRFs, including diabetes and glycaemic control, before initiation of chemotherapy. However, Alexandre et al. (European Heart Journal 2020) showed that only **42% of high-risk patients received the recommended cardioprotective therapy**, underlining a major gap in implementation; no Swiss data are currently available on this issue.

Two essential questions arise:
- Are CVRFs, particularly diabetes and related metabolic risk, systematically screened in oncology patients?
- During cardiology follow-up, are CVRFs—and especially diabetes—treated optimally and in line with current evidence on cardioprotective therapies?

### 1.3 Cardioprotective Potential of SGLT2 Inhibitors and Other Existing Therapies

Several drugs prescribed for the treatment of CVRFs have pleiotropic properties that may confer cardioprotective effects.

**SGLT2 inhibitors**, originally developed as glucose-lowering therapies for type 2 diabetes, are now established as core components of heart failure guideline-directed medical therapy and are recommended irrespective of diabetes status to reduce hospitalisation for heart failure and cardiovascular events. In large cardiovascular outcome and heart failure trials, empagliflozin and other SGLT2 inhibitors have consistently reduced the risk of heart failure hospitalisation and, in many settings, cardiovascular death. In the cardio-oncology context, robust preclinical data indicate that SGLT2 inhibitors exert myocardial metabolic reprogramming, mitochondrial protection, and anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic effects, with a **40–70% reduction in cardiac lesions** in rodent models exposed to cardiotoxic chemotherapy. Recent clinical and meta-analytic data in cancer patients with type 2 diabetes suggest that SGLT2 inhibitor use is associated with a substantially lower incidence of cancer therapy-related cardiac dysfunction, a reduction in all-cause mortality, and fewer heart failure hospitalisations. These findings position SGLT2 inhibitors as highly promising agents for cardio-oncology cardioprotection, particularly in diabetic patients exposed to anthracyclines and other cardiotoxic regimens.

**Statins** (for dyslipidaemia) exert anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anti-apoptotic, and endothelial protective effects. In a meta-analysis including 3,818 patients, Tini et al. (JACC CardioOncology 2022) reported a **44% reduction in the risk of cardiotoxicity** with statin therapy, and several randomised controlled trials (Acar 2011, Chotenimitkhun 2015, Nabati 2019) have yielded positive results. Statins will be analysed as important co-therapies.

## 2. Study Plan

### 2.1 Objectives

**Primary Objective:**
- To characterise the cardio-oncology population with a specific focus on the **prevalence and control of diabetes and other CVRFs**, and on cardiovascular pharmacotherapy with particular emphasis on **SGLT2 inhibitor use in patients with type 2 diabetes**.

**Secondary Objectives:**
1. Incidence of cardiotoxicity at 12 months
2. Quality of CVRF control, with detailed assessment of glycaemic control and optimisation of antidiabetic therapy
3. Adherence to the 2022 ESC cardio-oncology recommendations for CVRF management, including the use of cardioprotective agents in patients with diabetes
4. To identify patients with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) at baseline using the HFA-PEFF diagnostic score, and to describe the proportion of HFpEF patients in whom treatment with SGLT2 inhibitors would be indicated according to contemporary heart failure guidelines

**Hypothesis:**
- Among oncology patients with diabetes and dyslipidaemia, follow-up in a dedicated cardio-oncology clinic will be associated with improved control of CVRFs, increased utilisation of SGLT2 inhibitors in eligible diabetic patients, and an improved cardiovascular risk profile, potentially translating into lower cardiotoxicity rates.

### 2.2 Design and Population

**Study Type:** Retrospective and prospective observational cohort

**Inclusion Criteria:**
- Patients evaluated in the cardio-oncology clinic between January 2025 and December 2026
- Age ≥18 years
- Recent oncological diagnosis requiring evaluation and/or monitoring of cardiotoxicity

**Sample Size:**
- Inclusion of approximately 850–900 patients (500 retrospective in 2025 and 400 prospective in 2026) with follow-up until the end of 2026
- Assuming a 12–15% incidence of cardiotoxicity, this corresponds to approximately 100–130 events

### 2.3 Variables and Assessments

**Baseline:**
- Demographics, cancer characteristics (type, stage, treatment)
- CVRFs (hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, chronic kidney disease, obesity, family history) with **detailed characterisation of diabetes** (duration, complications, baseline HbA1c, antidiabetic therapy including SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, insulin)
- Established cardiovascular disease
- Echocardiography (LVEF, GLS)
- Biomarkers (troponin, NT-proBNP)
- ECG
- Cardiovascular and cardiorenal treatments (SGLT2 inhibitors, statins, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, beta-blockers, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists)
- Laboratory data (HbA1c, fasting glucose, lipid profile, creatinine/eGFR)
- **Application of the HFA-PEFF score**

At baseline, all patients will undergo systematic evaluation for suspected heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) using the HFA-PEFF diagnostic algorithm, integrating echocardiographic parameters (including GLS, left atrial volume index, left ventricular mass index, E/e′ ratio, and tricuspid regurgitation velocity) and natriuretic peptide levels. The HFA-PEFF score will be calculated for each patient, allowing classification into low (score <2), intermediate (score 2–4), or high likelihood (score >4) of HFpEF, with scores >4 considered diagnostic in line with haemodynamic validation studies. Among patients with confirmed or highly probable HFpEF (HFA-PEFF score >4) and preserved LVEF, we will identify those who fulfil guideline-based indications for SGLT2 inhibitor therapy, based on ESC 2023 heart failure recommendations, which grant a Class I level A recommendation to SGLT2 inhibitors to reduce heart failure hospitalisations and cardiovascular death in HFpEF. This will enable us to depict the HFpEF subset within the cardio-oncology cohort, quantify the therapeutic gap in SGLT2 inhibitor use, and explore the interaction between HFpEF, diabetes, SGLT2 inhibitor prescription, and subsequent cardiotoxicity and heart failure events.

**Follow-up (3–12 months):**
- Echocardiography and biomarkers
- Cardiovascular events
- Changes in anticancer treatment
- **Changes in antidiabetic and cardiovascular medications, with specific tracking of initiation, discontinuation, or dose adjustment of SGLT2 inhibitors**
- Hospitalisations and mortality

### 2.4 Endpoints

**Primary Endpoint:** Cardiotoxicity (ESC 2022 definition)
- Clinical cardiotoxicity: symptomatic heart failure or LVEF <50% with a ≥10% absolute decrease from baseline
- Subclinical cardiotoxicity: relative GLS reduction >15% from baseline

**Secondary Endpoints:**
- Major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE)
- Arrhythmias
- Modifications of anticancer treatment due to cardiac issues
- All-cause and cardiovascular mortality
- **In diabetic patients:** change in HbA1c, change in SGLT2 inhibitor prescription rates, and association between SGLT2 inhibitor use and cardiotoxicity incidence

### 2.5 Database

REDCap (institutional) with pseudonymisation

### 2.6 Statistical Analyses

- **Descriptive:** prevalence estimates with 95% confidence intervals, including prevalence of diabetes and SGLT2 inhibitor use
- **Comparative:** multivariable logistic regression (adjusted for age, sex, cancer type, treatment type, baseline LVEF, diabetes status, and SGLT2 inhibitor use)
- **Time-to-event analyses:** Kaplan–Meier curves and Cox proportional hazards models, including subgroup analyses by diabetes status and SGLT2 inhibitor exposure
- **Significance threshold:** p<0.05

### 2.7 Ethics

The study will be conducted in accordance with CER-VD approval, the Swiss Federal Human Research Act (LRH), and GDPR requirements. General institutional consent will be used for retrospective data, and written informed consent will be obtained for prospective participants.

### 2.8 Timeline

**Year 1 (requested funding):**
- Months 1–2: ethics approval, initiation of recruitment
- Months 3–8: retrospective data extraction, start of prospective enrolment
- Months 9–12: continuation of recruitment, quality control, preliminary analyses focusing on the diabetic subgroup and SGLT2 inhibitor utilisation
- **Deliverable:** database with ≥700 patients and protocol publication, including first descriptive data on diabetes, SGLT2 inhibitor use, and cardiotoxicity

**Year 2 (future funding):**
- Completion of 12-month follow-up, final analyses, and main manuscript preparation, with a central focus on diabetes and SGLT2 inhibitors

**Year 3 (future/institutional funding):**
- Secondary analyses (including interaction of SGLT2 inhibitor therapy with cancer type and treatment), and preparation of 2–3 additional publications

## 3. Importance of the Project

### 3.1 Originality

1. **First large-scale prospective Swiss cohort in cardio-oncology** with a dedicated focus on diabetes and SGLT2 inhibitor-based cardioprotection
2. **Real-world approach** reflecting complex routine clinical practice
3. **Emphasis on SGLT2 inhibitors** — a class of drugs with strong evidence in diabetes and heart failure and emerging, yet still under-explored, potential in cardio-oncology
4. **Embedded quality dimension** (guideline adherence and optimisation of cardiometabolic care)

### 3.2 Scientific Impact

**Expected contributions:**
- Contemporary Swiss epidemiological data in cardio-oncology, with detailed characterisation of diabetic patients and their management
- First prospective European data on SGLT2 inhibitor use and its association with cardiotoxicity in a real-world cardio-oncology setting
- Quantification of the therapeutic gap in SGLT2 inhibitor utilisation among eligible patients (diabetic and HFpEF)
- Identification of subgroups deriving greatest benefit from SGLT2 inhibitors in cardio-oncology

**Expected publications (5):**
1. Protocol publication (Trials/BMC Cardiovascular Disorders)
2. Main results (JACC: CardioOncology / European Heart Journal)
3. Diabetes and SGLT2 inhibitor focus (Diabetes Care / Diabetologia)
4. Statin focus (JACC: CardioOncology)
5. Guideline adherence and quality of care (European Journal of Heart Failure)

**Cumulative impact factor:** 80–150

### 3.3 Clinical and Public Health Impact

**Patient benefits:** Improved control of CVRFs and cardiovascular risk profile, with an expected decrease in cardiovascular events at medium and long term (to be confirmed in a future randomised trial).

### 3.4 Alignment with Boehringer Ingelheim's Mission

This project is fully aligned with Boehringer Ingelheim's commitment to advancing innovative, evidence-based therapies in diabetes and cardiovascular disease, and to exploring the broader potential of SGLT2 inhibitors in high-risk populations. The results will benefit thousands of oncology patients in Switzerland and beyond and will inform the design of future interventional trials of SGLT2 inhibitors in cardio-oncology.

## 5. Conclusion

The risk of cardiotoxicity and cardiovascular events is strongly influenced by the presence of CVRFs, with **diabetes representing a particularly high-risk condition** in cancer patients. Our project will determine whether CVRFs are adequately screened and optimally treated, with a particular focus on the utilisation of SGLT2 inhibitors in eligible patients.

This project is fully aligned with Boehringer Ingelheim's commitment to advancing innovative, evidence-based therapies in diabetes and cardiovascular disease, and to exploring the broader potential of SGLT2 inhibitors in high-risk populations. The results will benefit thousands of oncology patients in Switzerland and beyond and will inform the design of future interventional trials of SGLT2 inhibitors in cardio-oncology.
