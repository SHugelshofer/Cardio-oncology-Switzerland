## 1. Background

### 1.1 The Growing Challenge of Cardiovascular Toxicity in Oncology

Advances in oncology have transformed many cancers into chronic diseases: in Switzerland, more than 60% of patients survive beyond five years from diagnosis. This progress has uncovered a critical clinical challenge: the cardiovascular toxicity of anticancer treatments. The risk of dying from cardiovascular disease is doubled in cancer survivors compared with the general population, and the probability of developing heart failure is increased by 50% (*Nature*, 2020).

Anthracyclines, anti-HER2 agents, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, and immunotherapies induce cardiotoxicity ranging from subclinical dysfunction to severe heart failure. Our cardio-oncology outpatient clinic at CHUV (5–6 patients per day, 4–5 days per week since January 2025, approximately 1,000 patients per year) provides daily first-hand observation of these complications.

According to the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) guidelines on cardio-oncology (2022), the creation of cardio-oncology registries is strongly encouraged to monitor care quality and contribute to clinical research in this evidence-poor field.

### 1.2 Cardiovascular Risk Factors in the Oncological Population

Cardiovascular risk factors (CVRFs) play a key role and increase the risk of both cardiovascular disease and certain cancers. Cancer patients therefore frequently present with CVRFs, with prevalences that vary by cancer type and age distribution:

- **Diabetes mellitus:** 20–30%
- **Arterial hypertension:** 35–50%
- **Dyslipidaemia/hypercholesterolaemia:** 30–40%
- **Obesity:** 25–35%
- **Established cardiovascular disease:** 15–25%

*(Sources: CARDIOTOX Registry, N > 10,000 patients; EC3 European Study, N > 8,000 patients; Herrmann et al., Circ Res 2022; Sturgeon et al., EHJ 2019)*

These CVRFs substantially increase the risk of cardiotoxicity and strongly influence patients' overall prognosis. Patients with diabetes, for example, have a 2–3-fold higher risk of cardiac dysfunction under anthracyclines (Lipshultz et al., JCO 2012). Uncontrolled hypertension doubles the risk (Cardinale et al., JACC 2015).

Two critical questions emerge from daily clinical practice:

1. Are CVRFs systematically screened in cancer patients before and during cardiotoxic treatment?
2. During cardiological follow-up, are CVRFs treated to guideline-recommended targets?

### 1.3 The Particular Importance of Hypercholesterolaemia in Cardio-Oncology

Among all CVRFs present in the oncological population, **hypercholesterolaemia** occupies a particularly important place for the following reasons:

1. **High prevalence:** 30–40% of cancer patients present with significant dyslipidaemia (CARDIOTOX Registry; EC3 European Study).

2. **A modifiable risk factor:** Unlike age or genetics, hypercholesterolaemia is eminently treatable with well-established therapies (statins, ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitors).

3. **Interaction with cardiotoxicity:** Converging data indicate that hypercholesterolaemia aggravates myocardial injury induced by anthracyclines through inflammatory, oxidative, and endothelial mechanisms. Each additional CVRF, including dyslipidaemia, increases the risk of cardiotoxicity by 30–40% (Herrmann et al., Circulation 2016). Uncontrolled dyslipidaemia is an independent predictor of cardiotoxicity (HR 1.8; 95%CI 1.2–2.7; Cardinale et al., JACC 2015).

4. **Cardioprotective potential of statins:** Statins — the first-line treatment for hypercholesterolaemia — exert remarkable cardioprotective effects in cardio-oncology, well beyond their lipid-lowering action, through anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anti-apoptotic, endothelial-protective, and anti-fibrotic effects. The meta-analysis by Tini et al. (*JACC: CardioOncology*, 2022, N = 3,818 patients) showed a **44% reduction in the risk of cardiotoxicity** (RR 0.56; 95%CI 0.39–0.81).

5. **Undertreatment:** Available data suggest that fewer than 50% of hypercholesterolaemic cancer patients achieve their lipid targets according to ESC 2022 guidelines, notably because of the priority given to oncological treatments (Alexandre et al., *EHJ*, 2020: only 42% of high-risk patients received recommended cardiovascular therapy).

6. **New lipid-lowering therapies:** PCSK9 inhibitors (inclisiran, evolocumab, alirocumab) and bempedoic acid offer new opportunities, particularly inclisiran whose twice-yearly subcutaneous administration is particularly well-adapted to cancer patients whose oral treatment adherence may be compromised by complex therapeutic regimens.

### 1.4 Cardioprotective Medications: SGLT2 Inhibitors

Sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2i), prescribed for diabetes management, possess pleiotropic properties that are potentially cardioprotective in the context of cardiotoxic treatments:

- **Preclinical data:** Quagliariello et al. (2021): empagliflozin reduces myocardial fibrosis by 60% and preserves LVEF in doxorubicin-treated mice. Luo et al. (2022): dapagliflozin preserves mitochondrial function and reduces apoptosis by 55%.

- **Clinical data:** Gongora et al. (2022): 63% reduction in cardiotoxicity in 156 patients on anthracyclines treated with SGLT2i. Abdel-Qadir et al. (*JAMA Oncol*, 2023, N = 4,742 diabetic cancer patients): SGLT2i reduced heart failure hospitalisations by 35% compared with other antidiabetics (HR 0.65; 95%CI 0.48–0.88). Ongoing trials: EMPA-CARD (N = 300, end 2025), DAPA-PROTECT (N = 500, end 2026).

- **Gap:** No prospective European study in real-world conditions.

### 1.5 Cancers Sharing Risk Factors with Cardiovascular Disease

A particularly compelling angle of this research relates to **cancers that share the same risk factors as hypercholesterolaemia and metabolic syndrome**: colorectal cancer, post-menopausal breast cancer, endometrial cancer, lung cancer, renal cell carcinoma, hepatocellular carcinoma, pancreatic cancer, oesophageal adenocarcinoma, and prostate cancer. In these patients, active treatment of CVRFs could reduce cardiovascular risk and potentially also influence oncological prognosis through the pleiotropic effects of cardiovascular medications.

### 1.6 Current Evidence Gaps and Justification

Despite the accumulating evidence, important gaps remain:

- No Swiss prospective real-world data on CVRF prevalence and management quality in cardio-oncology
- No data on the association between LDL-C target achievement (per ESC 2022) and cardiotoxicity in a contemporary European cohort
- No prospective European data on the use of new lipid-lowering therapies (PCSK9i, inclisiran) in the oncological context
- Limited real-world data on the effect of SGLT2i and statins in European cardio-oncology populations
- No Swiss data on adherence to ESC 2022 cardio-oncology guidelines

**This registry directly addresses these gaps.** It will provide the first prospective Swiss evidence base, directly applicable to clinical practice and guideline development.

## 2. Objectives

### 2.1 Objectives

**Primary Objective:**  
To describe the characteristics of the cardio-oncology population at CHUV, including the prevalence and management quality of cardiovascular risk factors (with particular focus on hypercholesterolaemia), cardiovascular treatments prescribed, and adherence to ESC 2022 cardio-oncology guideline recommendations.

**Secondary Objectives:**

1. To determine the 12-month incidence of cardiotoxicity (defined by ESC 2022 criteria) in this population
2. To assess the quality of CVRF control (hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, obesity) during cardiological follow-up
3. To quantify the association between LDL-cholesterol target achievement (LDL-C < 1.4 mmol/L or < 1.8 mmol/L according to cardiovascular risk level per ESC 2022) and the occurrence of cardiotoxicity at 12 months
4. To evaluate therapeutic modifications induced by specialist cardiological follow-up (initiation, intensification, or substitution of cardiovascular treatments)
5. To analyse the impact of lipid-lowering treatment intensity (low/moderate/high-intensity statin ± ezetimibe ± PCSK9 inhibitor) on cardiotoxicity
6. To explore variations in the lipid profile (LDL-C, HDL-C, triglycerides, Lp(a)) during oncological treatment and cardiological follow-up
7. To assess major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), arrhythmias, treatment modifications, and all-cause mortality
8. To evaluate the adherence to prescribed cardiovascular medications (including lipid-lowering, antidiabetic, and antihypertensive treatments)
9. To identify factors associated with failure to achieve lipid targets (cancer type, oncological treatment, drug interactions, adverse effects)

**Hypotheses:**

1. Patients with diabetes and dyslipidaemia demonstrate improved CVRF control and cardiovascular risk profiles during cardiological follow-up in the cardio-oncology clinic compared with baseline
2. LDL-C target achievement per ESC 2022 is associated with a reduced risk of cardiotoxicity at 12 months
3. At inclusion, fewer than 50% of hypercholesterolaemic cancer patients achieve their lipid targets per ESC 2022
4. Cardiological follow-up leads to a statistically significant improvement in lipid management (initiation or intensification of treatment)

### 2.2 Endpoints

**Primary endpoint:**  
Cardiotoxicity at 12 months, defined according to ESC 2022 criteria:
- *Clinical cardiotoxicity:* New symptomatic heart failure OR LVEF < 50% with a drop of ≥10 percentage points from baseline
- *Subclinical cardiotoxicity:* Reduction of Global Longitudinal Strain (GLS) > 15% from baseline

**Secondary endpoints:**

| Endpoint | Definition / Measurement |
|---|---|
| LDL-C target achievement | Proportion of hypercholesterolaemic patients achieving LDL-C < 1.4 mmol/L (very high CV risk) or < 1.8 mmol/L (high CV risk) per ESC 2022 at 6 months |
| Absolute LDL-C change | ΔLDL-C (mmol/L) from baseline to 6 and 12 months |
| MACE | Composite of cardiovascular death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, non-fatal stroke, hospitalisation for heart failure |
| Arrhythmia | New atrial fibrillation, significant QTc prolongation (> 500 ms), ventricular arrhythmia |
| Oncological treatment modification | Dose reduction, temporary interruption, or definitive discontinuation due to cardiovascular toxicity |
| All-cause mortality | Death from any cause during follow-up |
| CV hospitalisation | Any unplanned hospitalisation for cardiovascular cause |
| Biomarker elevation | High-sensitivity troponin I/T or NT-proBNP elevation above institutional upper limits |
| Treatment initiation/intensification | Proportion of hypercholesterolaemic patients with new or intensified lipid-lowering therapy following cardiological evaluation |
| CVRF control at 12 months | HbA1c < 7% (diabetics); SBP < 130 mmHg (hypertensives) |

### 2.3 Independent Variables

**Primary variable of interest (CVRF-HYPERCHOL sub-study):**  
LDL-C target achievement at 6 months (binary: yes/no per ESC 2022 thresholds), as the exposure variable for the cardiotoxicity outcome.

**Primary variable of interest (CVRF-SGLT2i analysis):**  
SGLT2 inhibitor use at baseline and during follow-up (binary: yes/no) in diabetic patients.

**Adjustment variables (pre-specified confounders):**
- Age and sex
- Cancer type and stage (TNM)
- Type of cardiotoxic treatment (anthracyclines: yes/no, cumulative dose; anti-HER2: yes/no; thoracic radiotherapy: yes/no, dose; tyrosine kinase inhibitors: yes/no; immunotherapy: yes/no)
- Baseline LVEF
- Number of CVRFs at baseline
- Baseline renal function (eGFR)
- Baseline LVEF and GLS

## 3. Design

**Study type:** Prospective observational cohort study with a retrospective component

**Setting:** Cardio-oncology Outpatient Clinic, Department of Cardiology, Lausanne University Hospital (CHUV), Lausanne, Switzerland. This clinic is unique in French-speaking Switzerland, seeing approximately 1,000 patients per year since January 2025.

**Study structure:**
- **Retrospective component:** Data collected from January 2025 to December 2025 (approximately 500 patients already registered in REDCap) will be analysed retrospectively after ethics committee approval.
- **Prospective component:** New patients attending the cardio-oncology clinic from 2026 will be enrolled prospectively (approximately 400 patients in 2026).
- **Follow-up:** All patients will be followed for up to 12 months with evaluations at 3, 6, and 12 months.

**Collection period:** January 2025 – December 2026 (data collection); follow-up analysis through December 2027.

**Note on data de-identification risk:** All analyses will use coded (pseudo-anonymised) data. No direct patient identifiers will be used in the research database. Risk of re-identification is considered low, given that the research data will be handled exclusively by authorised CHUV personnel with appropriate data security measures in place.

**Evaluation methods / techniques used:**
- Clinical cardiology examination (medical history, physical examination)
- Transthoracic echocardiography (LVEF by modified Simpson's biplane method; GLS by 2D speckle tracking)
- 12-lead electrocardiography
- Blood sampling: high-sensitivity troponin I/T, NT-proBNP, HbA1c, complete lipid profile (LDL-C, HDL-C, total cholesterol, triglycerides, non-HDL-C, Lp(a), ApoB/ApoA1), creatinine/eGFR, ALAT/ASAT, CK, TSH, cortisol (where clinically indicated)
- Standardised electronic Case Report Forms in REDCap (CHUV institutional installation)
- Structured collection of oncological treatment data (type, dose, schedule) from medical records

## 4. Origin of the Data

**Data source:** Institutional cardio-oncology registry at CHUV, implemented in REDCap (Research Electronic Data Capture), operational since January 2025.

**Data provenance:** All data originate from routine clinical consultations at the CHUV Cardio-Oncology Outpatient Clinic. Data were/are collected as part of standard clinical care and recorded in both the institutional electronic medical record (Soarian, CHUV) and the REDCap research registry by clinicians and authorised research staff.

**Nature of data used:**
- Health-related personal data (coded for analysis)
- Demographic data (age, sex, weight, height)
- Cancer data (type, histology, stage, treatment received)
- Cardiovascular data (medical history, echocardiographic measurements, ECG, biomarkers, blood pressure)
- Biological data (lipid profile, glycaemic parameters, renal function, liver enzymes, thyroid function)
- Medication data (cardiovascular treatments, oncological treatments, comedications)
- Outcome data (cardiovascular events, hospitalisations, treatment modifications, death)

**No biological material (tissue, blood samples, biobank samples) is used for this study.** All analyses are based exclusively on health-related personal data collected during routine clinical care.

**Population studied:** Adult patients (≥18 years) with an oncological diagnosis who are referred for cardiotoxicity evaluation and/or monitoring at the CHUV Cardio-Oncology Outpatient Clinic. This population is not considered a vulnerable population as defined by Swiss legislation; however, all patients have active cancer, which is taken into account in the ethical assessment.

**Relevance of this population for the scientific question:** This population directly represents the patients for whom the scientific questions are most relevant: cancer patients receiving potentially cardiotoxic treatments, in whom CVRF management is the central variable of interest. The population is well-characterised clinically and is the appropriate population for answering the study hypotheses.

## 8. Scientific Methods and Sample Size

### 8.1 Sample Size

**Total cohort:** Approximately **850–900 patients** over the study period (January 2025 – December 2026):
- Retrospective component: ~500 patients (2025 data already collected in REDCap)
- Prospective component: ~350–400 patients (new enrolments in 2026)

**CVRF-HYPERCHOL sub-study:** Approximately **250–350 patients** with documented hypercholesterolaemia (estimated prevalence 30–40% of the total cohort).

**Sample size justification:**
- Estimated 12-month incidence of cardiotoxicity: 12–15% (approximately 100–130 events in the total cohort)
- With ~850 patients and ~100–130 events, the study is adequately powered (>80%) to detect a relative risk reduction of 40–50% in the primary analyses after multivariate adjustment
- For the HYPERCHOL sub-study: ~30–50 events in the ~300 hypercholesterolaemic patients, sufficient power for the primary analysis (LDL-C target achievement and cardiotoxicity)
- All calculations assume a two-sided alpha of 0.05

### 8.2 Statistical Methods

**Descriptive statistics:**
- Continuous variables: mean ± standard deviation or median (IQR) according to distribution
- Categorical variables: frequencies and percentages with 95% confidence intervals
- Prevalence of CVRFs at baseline (with 95%CI)
- Distribution of lipid profiles, LDL-C values, and proportion achieving ESC 2022 targets
- Frequency of each cardiovascular treatment class

**Analyses for primary endpoint (cardiotoxicity at 12 months):**
- Logistic regression (unadjusted and multivariate):
  - Dependent variable: cardiotoxicity at 12 months (yes/no)
  - Primary exposure: LDL-C target achievement at 6 months (HYPERCHOL sub-study) or SGLT2i use (diabetic sub-analysis)
  - Adjustment variables (pre-specified): age, sex, cancer type, anthracyclines (cumulative dose), anti-HER2, thoracic radiotherapy, baseline LVEF, arterial hypertension, diabetes, eGFR
- Propensity score matching (PSM) to reduce selection bias in the comparison of treated versus untreated patients (statins, SGLT2i)

**Analyses for secondary endpoints:**
- Time-to-event analyses: Kaplan-Meier survival curves, log-rank test, Cox proportional hazards models for time to cardiotoxicity and MACE
- Longitudinal analyses: linear mixed models for repeated echocardiographic and biological measurements (LVEF, GLS, LDL-C, HbA1c) over the 3, 6, and 12-month follow-up visits
- Mediation analysis: to decompose the cardioprotective effect of statins into LDL-C-mediated and pleiotropic (LDL-C-independent) components
- Subgroup analyses (pre-specified): by cancer type, anthracycline use, LDL-C quartiles at baseline, presence of additional CVRFs, statin intensity

**Missing data:** Multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE) for missing data in secondary variables; complete case analysis for primary endpoint.

**Significance level:** p < 0.05 (two-sided) for all pre-specified analyses.

**Software:** R (version ≥ 4.3) and Stata (version ≥ 18). All analyses will be performed by a qualified biostatistician from the CHUV Clinical Research Unit.

### 8.3 Potential Limitations and Risk of Bias

1. **Selection bias:** The cardio-oncology clinic receives patients referred by oncologists, which may over-represent patients with higher cardiovascular risk or known CVRFs. Comparison with general oncology populations will be performed.
2. **Observational design:** As an observational registry, causal inferences regarding CVRF treatment effects cannot be definitively established. Propensity score methods and sensitivity analyses will be used to minimise confounding.
3. **Loss to follow-up:** Patients may be lost to follow-up due to disease progression, transfer to palliative care, or death unrelated to cardiovascular causes. Competing risk analyses will be performed where appropriate.
4. **Lipid data variability:** Oncological treatments may themselves alter lipid profiles (corticosteroids, mTOR inhibitors, androgen deprivation therapy), potentially confounding the LDL-C exposure variable. These interactions will be documented and controlled for in sensitivity analyses.
5. **Reproducibility:** All echocardiographic measurements will be performed according to standardised ASE/EACVI protocols. Inter-observer variability will be assessed.

## 10. Data Protection

**Coding of data:**  
All health-related personal data used in this research project are **coded (pseudo-anonymised)**. Each patient is assigned a unique anonymous participant identification number (e.g., CVRF-001, CVRF-002, …). This code replaces all direct identifiers (name, date of birth, hospital record number, address) in the research database.

**Key management:**  
The coding key (linking participant ID to real patient identifiers) is held **exclusively by Dr Sarah Hugelshofer** (project leader) and is stored in a **password-protected, encrypted file on the CHUV secured institutional server** (separate from the REDCap research database). Access to the key is restricted to Dr Hugelshofer and, under her supervision, authorised CHUV research staff. The key is never transferred outside CHUV.

**Data access:**  
Project data will be handled with utmost discretion and are only accessible to authorised personnel who require the data to fulfil their duties within the scope of the research project. On Case Report Forms (CRFs), participants are identified only by their participant ID. No personal identifiers are present in the REDCap research database.

**Data platform:**  
The REDCap institutional installation at CHUV is used as the research database. REDCap is hosted on CHUV-managed servers located in Switzerland. The platform is ISO 27001-certified and compliant with Swiss data protection law (nDSG/LPD) and GDPR. Role-based access controls are configured such that each team member can access only the data necessary for their function.

**Data transfer:**  
No transfer of identifiable or coded research data outside CHUV or Switzerland is planned. Statistical analyses will be performed on de-identified aggregated datasets on CHUV secure infrastructure.

**Data linkage:**  
Data linkage between the research database and the CHUV electronic health record (Soarian) is performed exclusively for data extraction purposes, under the supervision of the project leader. After extraction, no ongoing direct linkage to identifiable clinical records is maintained in the research database.

**Technical and organisational measures:**  
- All digital documents are password-protected
- Paper CRFs (if used) are stored in locked cabinets within the CHUV Department of Cardiology research office
- Access to the REDCap database is controlled by user-level authentication (individual username and password)
- Audit trail functionality is enabled in REDCap (all data entries and modifications are logged)
- Regular encrypted backups are performed on CHUV institutional servers

**Note on biological material:** This project does not involve the use, storage, or processing of any biological material (tissue, blood samples, biobank samples). All data used are health-related personal data collected during routine clinical care.

- Research data in REDCap will be **anonymised** (irreversible removal of the participant ID by destruction of the coding key) or archived in anonymised form on CHUV institutional servers

- Paper documents containing personal data will be **securely shredded**
- Anonymised research data will be retained for the required statutory period (see Section 12) and may subsequently be shared with qualified researchers as anonymised datasets, in accordance with open science principles

## 13. Ethical and Regulatory Requirements

### 13.1 Risk-Benefit Assessment

**Expected benefits:**

*Scientific benefits:*
- First prospective Swiss registry in cardio-oncology with comprehensive CVRF characterisation
- First Swiss data on LDL-C target achievement and its association with cardiotoxicity in cancer patients
- Validation (or refutation) of preclinical and small-RCT evidence on cardioprotective medications in real-world conditions
- Direct contribution to ESC guideline development in cardio-oncology
- Expected 5 publications in high-impact cardiology/oncology journals (JACC: CardioOncology, European Heart Journal, Diabetes Care)

*Clinical benefits:*
- Improved management of cardiovascular risk factors in cancer patients at CHUV through the structured monitoring inherent to registry participation
- Generation of evidence to guide clinical decisions for the thousands of cancer patients treated annually in Switzerland
- Potential reduction in cardiovascular events if improved CVRF management is demonstrated

*Societal benefits:*
- Data directly applicable to national and international clinical practice guidelines
- Foundation for future randomised controlled trials
- Training of future cardiologists in evidence-based cardio-oncology practice

**Data risks and their prevention:**

The study involves the use of coded health-related personal data. The main risks are:
1. **Risk of re-identification:** Mitigated by strict pseudo-anonymisation, secure key management, restricted data access, and use of CHUV-hosted infrastructure (see Section 10).
2. **Risk of data breach:** Mitigated by REDCap's institutional security framework, encrypted storage, and password-protected access.
3. **Risk of stigmatisation or discrimination:** Low. The data concern common medical conditions and do not involve genetic data, psychiatric diagnoses, or information that could lead to social discrimination.

**Harm assessment:**  
The risk of harm to participants is considered **minimal**: no invasive procedures are performed as part of this research; all data collection occurs within routine clinical care. The risk of re-identification, while not zero, is low given the implemented safeguards. No additional blood sampling, imaging, or procedures are required beyond those already performed as part of standard clinical care.

**Proportionality:**  
The expected scientific and clinical benefits substantially outweigh the minimal risks to participants. The study addresses scientifically important questions that cannot be answered by other means (e.g., existing published datasets do not include Swiss real-world cardio-oncology data).

**Liability:**  
The project leader (Dr Sarah Hugelshofer) and the institution (CHUV) bear responsibility for all aspects of data handling and data security within the scope of this protocol. No separate research insurance is required for this type of observational study.

The results will be scientifically valid and generalisable to comparable European cardio-oncology populations, with the caveat that this is a single-centre Swiss study. The prospective design, standardised data collection, and pre-specified analysis plan minimise risk of bias. Limitations (Section 8.3) will be thoroughly discussed in publications.
